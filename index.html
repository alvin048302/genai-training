Module 1
Glossary

Five terms you'll hear everywhere

Quick definitions — so the jargon doesn't slow you down.

LLM
Large Language Model — the brain behind tools like ChatGPT and Claude.
Prompt
What you type in. Better question = better answer.
Token
A chunk of text the AI reads — roughly 4 characters, or about 75% of an average English word. AI services charge per token, and each conversation has a token limit, so longer prompts cost more and can get cut off.
Hallucination
When AI sounds confident but is making things up.
Training Cutoff
The date after which the AI doesn't know what's happened — like a friend who's been on a remote island.
You don't need all five fluently — just to know what they mean when they come up
🗣️
Analogy

Like learning five basic phrases before travelling to a new country — you won't be fluent, but you'll understand what's going on around you. These five terms unlock 90% of any AI conversation.

Module 3
Risk #1 — The big one

Hallucination

When AI sounds completely confident — but is making things up. The hardest risk to spot, because it doesn't feel like a mistake.

Q
You ask
"Who won the 1987 Best Author award?"
AI
AI confidently replies
"It was Margaret Thompson for her novel The Hidden Garden, published in 1986."
⚠ The award and book don't exist
Sounds completely plausible. Completely invented.
🎓
Analogy

Like a student who never says "I don't know". Instead of admitting it, they invent a confident-sounding answer. The danger isn't that they sound unsure — it's that they sound completely sure.

✅

The fix

Never trust AI for facts that matter — names, dates, numbers, regulations. Always verify from a reliable source before acting.

Module 3
The variability property

Same question, different answer

AI is probabilistic — and that creates real governance challenges in workplaces.

⚙️ Deterministic
Traditional software
Calculator
Database query
Excel formula
Same input → same output
Predictable. Auditable.
🎲 Probabilistic
Generative AI
ChatGPT
Claude
Gemini
Same input → different output
Creative. Variable. Harder to audit.
Almost all business software is deterministic — Gen AI breaks that assumption
📋

Reproducibility

Can't replay the exact same output from yesterday — affects audit trails and compliance.

🔍

Quality control

Output is a range, not a value. Testing AI is judging "good enough", not "right or wrong".

🎭

Brand consistency

Without guardrails, customers can get different tones from the same prompt across your team.

❓

Decision auditability

"Why did the AI suggest this?" — even the same prompt may produce different reasoning.

🍳
Analogy

Like a skilled chef vs a vending machine. The vending machine gives you exactly what's labelled, every time — predictable, but limited. The chef adapts to what's fresh that day — creative, but each plate looks slightly different. The variation is what makes AI useful. The variation is also what makes it harder to govern.

✅

The fix

Tight prompts. Human review. Documented use cases. Don't outlaw AI for what it is — design your processes around what it does.

Module 3
Risks 2–5

Four more limits to remember

Each one is simple — but together they shape how to use AI well.

⏰ Outdated

AI doesn't know what's happened since its training cutoff.

Like a friend just back from a long isolated trip.

⚖️ Biased

It learned from human writing — and absorbed our biases.

Reflects whatever was common in its training, not what's fair.

🧠 Habit-forming

The more AI does for you, the less your own skills get used.

Use it to work faster — not to stop thinking.

🔒 Privacy

Anything you type in may be stored or used in future training.

Treat it like a public space, not a private chat.
Each has a simple analogy — that's how to remember them
📖
Analogy

Like a brilliant encyclopaedia from a few years ago. Most of what's inside is still true. Some of it is out of date. Some of it reflects the perspective of who wrote it. You wouldn't copy and paste from it without thinking — you'd use it as a starting point, and verify what matters.
